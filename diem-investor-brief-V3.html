§ 01 · Executive Summary

The Bottom Line

Three numbers decide this allocation. You only need to believe one of them to act.

For a $1,000 deployment in April 2026, the highest-yield Venice exposure is the stack play: stake VVV, mint a portion to DIEM for predictable API utility, retain the rest for full emission yield. This captures both a USD-denominated utility yield ($416/year if 100% utilized) and a 14.4-18% VVV emission yield simultaneously. Combined risk-adjusted three-year terminal value lands near $1,953 on $1,000 if the API is actively consumed, against $1,602 for pure TAO staking with no counterparty risk.

The headline finding is structural, not numerical. VVV staking and DIEM minting are not alternatives but components of the same Venice-exposed position; treating them as competing instruments was the analytical error in earlier drafts. For Venice exposure that does not consume the API, TAO at 17% with fair-launch tokenomics structurally dominates VVV at 18% with corporate-counterparty tokenomics, because the Venice counterparty discount more than offsets the marginal yield difference once survival probability is applied.

For Ice-ninja's specific use case, where daily inference consumption is genuine and not theoretical, a barbell allocation captures both: TAO for protocol-grade yield, the VVV+DIEM stack for Venice utility, and a stablecoin tail for optionality. The recommended split is detailed in §06.

§ 02 · Methodology

Survival Probability, Decomposed

Base rate plus signed adjustments. Subjective but transparent. Every line is contestable.

The survival question reduces to: what is the probability Venice the company is still honoring DIEM redemptions for $1/day at the end of year three? Base rates draw from three reference classes. Crypto projects with $100M+ market cap reaching three years of operation post-launch run roughly 75% based on CoinMarketCap historical attrition. Series-A-funded software companies hit five-year survival around 50%. The intersection (well-capitalized crypto with real revenue) lands around 75% three-year, 50% five-year as the prior. Then Venice-specific adjustments shift the estimate up or down.

§ 03 · Expected Value

The Decision Tree

Each scenario weighted by probability. Principal recovery and yield realization separated.

The decision tree below compares the two Venice-exposed strategies on equal footing. Path A (DIEM-only) buys raw DIEM at $1,051 for predictable USD-denominated API utility. Path B (VVV+DIEM stack) buys 111 VVV at $8.96, stakes all of it, mints one DIEM by locking 90 VVV (capturing 14.4% emissions on the locked portion plus 18% on the unlocked remainder), and consumes the API utility. Both paths share the 70% three-year Venice survival probability. Each branch terminal shows total economic value extracted, weighted by branch probability, summed for expected value.

§ 07 · Framework

The Decision Ladder

Five questions, in order. Answer the first one honestly and most others resolve themselves.

Q1 · Will you actually consume $1/day of Venice inference? If no: skip DIEM entirely. Stake VVV directly for 18% if Venice exposure is desired, or skip Venice and go to TAO. If yes, proceed.
Q2 · Is the inference need privacy-sensitive? If no: OpenRouter is 20-40% cheaper per token. Don't pay the Venice markup unless privacy matters. If yes, DIEM utility yield is real. Proceed.
Q3 · How much can you tolerate losing if Venice fails? Multiply DIEM allocation by 0.30 (failure probability). That number is your expected loss. Size accordingly. Never exceed what hurts but doesn't destroy.
Q4 · Can you commit to daily utilization for three years? Underutilization halves the EV. Set up automated batch workflows to drain the daily allocation, or accept the lower yield in your model.
Q5 · What's your exit plan if a survival risk materializes? Burning DIEM unlocks the original sVVV. Unstaking VVV takes 7 days. Have a kill-switch defined: e.g., founder departure or revenue drop ≥40% triggers burn-and-exit.
"The risk is real. VVV has a $400M cap, Venice is a centralized counterparty, and the DIEM market has an $80–$1,191 price history, meaning 15× range. Position-size small, stack thoughtfully, treat the whole thing as utility-cost-negation rather than pure yield farming." From the Sliither Briefing · Iteration v1
